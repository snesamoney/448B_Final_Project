Silent Cues in the Human Voice: Acoustic Biomarkers for Parkinson’s Disease

Authors: Gavin Liu, Sean Nesamoney, Ngoc Tran

Introduction

It is estimated that nearly 1 million people in the United States are living with Parkinson’s Disease (PD) (National Institute of Neurological Disorders and Stroke).

While Parkinson’s Disease is most often recognized by its visible motor symptoms and slowness of movement, one of its earliest and most subtle indicators is often heard rather than seen. The human voice is a complex instrument produced by the precise coordination of airflow and muscle control. For patients with PD, the neurological degradation that affects limb movement also impacts the larynx and vocal folds, leading to a condition known as dysphonia. This typically manifests as a softer, breathy, or monotonic voice, often appearing years before an actual diagnosis of PD. This article explores the potential of using these subtle acoustic changes as digital biomarkers. By analyzing a dataset of voice recordings from both healthy individuals and diagnosed patients, we look at distinct human voice biomarkers such as pitch instability (jitter), loudness variation (shimmer), and many other biomarkers to see the effectiveness of these voice biomarkers to detect the PD.

To understand the data, we will first look at the biology behind PD. Vocal cords are delicate folds of tissue that vibrate thousands of times per second to produce sound. In a healthy voice, this vibration is periodic and stable. However, PD causes patients to have a lack of fine motor control in their laryngeal muscles. This instability leaves a mathematical footprint in the voice signal, presenting itself as useful acoustic biomarkers to analyze. The dataset used in this article consists of various acoustic measurements categorized into groups such as Jitter (frequency variation), Shimmer (amplitude variation), and certain non-linear measurements. For example, a healthy voice holds a steady pitch, while a PD voice may have higher jitter from fluctuating microscopically from cycle to cycle.

How Parkinson’s Disrupts Vocal Stability and Volume

Parkinson’s disease negatively impacts the delicate muscles of the larynx, causing vocal instability. In research, this is referred to as jitter, the cycle-to-cycle variation in the pitch (fundamental frequency) of a person's voice, caused by irregularities in vocal fold vibration. This is largely driven by the rigidity, stiffness, and tremor of the laryngeal muscles in the presence of Parkinson's disease, causing more irregular and erratic oscillations in pitch. Consequently, people with Parkinson's often exhibit higher jitter, which can result in a rough or harsh vocal quality.

Similarly, these disrupted muscles also impact the control an individual has over the volume of their voice. In research, this is referred to as shimmer, the cycle-to-cycle fluctuations in the amplitude of the voice. Just as jitter is often used as a metric to assess the progression of PD, shimmer can be used in the same way. PD patients tend to exhibit higher-than-normal shimmer because the disease disrupts the physiological mechanisms that control voice volume.

Finding Other Interesting Biomarkers via Scatterplots

While a single time series illustration helps highlight how irregularities appear and change over time, Parkinson’s cannot be diagnosed from a couple of biomarkers alone. We instead look for patterns that repeat across many different biomarkers. To better understand these diverse signals, we shift from a temporal view to a multivariate perspective. One way to discover these patterns is through scatterplots of two features on each of the X-axis and Y-axis.

For Example...

The plot below initially shows a scatterplot of PPE (Pitch Period Entropy) and Harmonics-to-Noise Ratio (HNR) across both healthy subjects and patients with PD.

Both PPE and HNR measures capture complementary dimensions of vocal degradation. PPE rises when pitch becomes unstable and irregular, while HNR falls as the voice loses its harmonic structure and becomes noisier. When plotted together, these opposing effects create a clear separation: Healthy voices cluster tightly in the low-PPE, high-HNR region and patients with PD have voices shifting toward higher PPE and lower HNR.

PPE and HNR are just one example of a particularly strong pairing, but not the only features worth exploring. By experimenting with other combinations of features, you may uncover pairings that produce even sharper boundaries or reveal more nuanced patterns between healthy subjects and those with PD. Rather than treating this exploration as a final answer, it acts as a compass that points us toward which biomarkers are worth deeper investigation. Exploring Individual Vocal Biomarkers

Having surveyed the broader feature space, we now turn our attention to the biomarkers themselves. Instead of looking at features two at a time, this section isolates each one to examine its distribution and overall diagnostic value. By focusing on individual features, we can see which vocal biomarkers consistently differ between healthy and Parkinson’s speakers. This microscopic view can help explain why certain combinations stand out in the earlier scatterplots.

The interactive visualization below allows you to explore the diagnostic power of these different vocal features. In the lower distribution plot, each dot represents a person. The goal of a diagnostic test is to separate these two groups as best as possible. By dragging the threshold slider, the vertical black line is adjusted, which acts as the decision boundary when evaluating the effectiveness of a vocal feature. A highly effective feature such as PPE or RPDE, a non-linear feature, will show a distinct separation between the green and red clusters, allowing the threshold line to split them with minimal error.

An effective way to measure the effectiveness of the classification is to plot a Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) Curve. It visualizes the trade-off between catching the disease and avoiding false positives. As you drag the slider to change the threshold decision line, the black marker on the ROC curve moves along the blue curve. Moving the threshold to catch more patients increases the True Positive Rate but comes at the cost of incorrectly diagnosing more healthy people to be sick, which increases the False Positive Rate.

The measure of a feature's success is the ROC's AUC (Area Under the Curve), displayed on the bottom right. An AUC of 0.5 suggests the feature is no better than a coin flip, while an AUC of 1.0 represents a perfect test. By exploring different features in the dropdown menu, you can see that while basic pitch measurements often struggle to distinguish the groups, other non-linear measurements like PPE (Pitch Period Entropy) have some of the strongest predictive power for PD, which is aligned with the feature-pair classification in the visualization above as well.

Conclusion

In the end, the patterns across these visualizations reveal a subtle but consistent story: Parkinson’s disease alters vocal behavior in ways that become visible when we examine the right biomarkers. Features like PPE, HNR, and jitter-based metrics repeatedly emerge as strong indicators, providing clearer separation between healthy and PD patients.

By experimenting with new feature combinations, more patterns or subtle subclusters within the PD and healthy groups are still left to be discovered. The work here illustrates how the “silent cues” of the human voice can become powerful digital biomarkers for earlier and more precise detection of Parkinson’s disease.
